Our idea for cleaning the surface of the seas from oil pollution could have been implemented as early as 2011 when we entered the top 10 with our project 'Artificial Tornado.'

The disaster that occurred in April 2010 on the Deepwater Horizon oil platform turned out to be the largest environmental catastrophe in the history of the United States. The oil leak was only stopped by August 4. As a result of the accident, 11 people died, and about 5 million barrels of crude oil spilled into the waters of the Gulf of Mexico.

The X-Prize Foundation, known for its competitions among scientists and engineers with large prize funds, announced a competition for the best method of cleaning the seas from spilled oil, with a prize fund of $1.4 million.

For this competition, we presented a project with an artificial tornado controlled by electric current. As early as 1917, physicist Robertson Rector conducted a similar experiment. Our idea was to create an artificial water tornado using electromagnetic fields, where the tornado would draw spilled oil from the surface. The oil within the tornado could be filtered similar to a vacuum cleaner. Additionally, this technology could be applied to collect household waste from the surface. The efficiency of this technology would not only allow for cleaning the seas from major pollutants but also address the issue of water blooms, with the collected waste being repurposed as mineral fertilizers. Such surface cleaning would contribute to the fight against global climate change, considering that the seas and oceans are the primary sources of the Earth's oxygen.
